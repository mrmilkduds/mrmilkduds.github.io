Thermodynamic Energy Storage System

This page serves as an introduction to the senior design project I've been working on as Simulations & Modeling lead since September of 2021 in collaboration with ten of my peers. It covers the general concept of our design as well as my individual contributions to our effort. We are just now completeing our Critical Design Review and will start manufacturing and testing in the coming weeks.

Overview

The focus of our senior design class was energy storage systems that have applications in space exploration. On Earth we have the luxuries of infrastructure and raw materials that allows for the several hundred gigawatts of power able to be stored - 90% of which is stored in the form of pumped-storage hydroelectricity. The places humans would like to colonize in space, think the Moon or Mars, do not yet have these type of luxuries. Space also has another problem.

Managing heat is a notoriously tricky thing in space. It's why the International Space Station has massive radiators and why the newly launched James Webb Telescope has a 70-foot-long sunshield. And why our team decided to focus on a device that would not only manage heat, but also use it as a method of energy storage.

In a space application, the TESS would be used on a lunar or martian colony in a fashion similar to the way the heat rejection system on the ISS works. Instead of radiating heat from electronics or solar radiation back to space, however, the heat would instead be transfered to the TESS. During periods of high thermal load, like a lunar 'day', the TESS would soak up this energy and be used during,in this example, a lunar 'night' to produce energy and dissipate stored heat.

Integrated System Model

The majority of my work has been as Keeper of the Integrated Model. This includes managing source control, verification & validation, and, primarily, working with technical subsystem leads on their mathmatical models, implimenting them, and integrating them into a cohesive script that accurately models the behavior of our system.

My initial goal for the model was for it to serve also as a design tool for the team. This meant tractable values for different components of the system, an easy-to-use and robust GUI so the technical leads wouldn't have to muck around in the code, and easily accesibly outputs. To meet these requirements I decided to code the first iteration of the model in Python.

The pictures above are the shell of the inregrated model GUI. It featured a Save/Load function that allowed for different model presets to be analyzed quickly. The model itself was object-oriented, which allowed for fairly tractable values, allowing for the capability to selectively analyze different values of various system components on the GUI - something that did wonders to keep the user's monitor from looking like something out of War Games. I resolved rather quickly, however, that the use of Python was, while idyllic in some cases, not very practical in most. Much of my team had very little or no Python experience and the design and models at this point in time were changing very rapidly. This lead to a significant bottleneck in our development process, where most changes to the integrated model had to be done by myself.

We switched to MATLAB, a language everyone on the team had used at some point or the other. The turn around times on changes to our design or model were much shorter, as separate technical leads could change sections of their code at will, or provide scripts for me to implement in the integrated system model.
